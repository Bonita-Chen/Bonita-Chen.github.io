Nowadays, hypertension and diabetes have become major chronic diseases of concern in public health, affecting the daily lives of many people. As a result, knowing how to effectively screen and prevent these conditions is important. During this process, BMI has served as a widely used screening measure.

Body Mass Index, hereafter abbreviated as BMI, is a well-known concept proposed by Belgian statistician Adolphe Quetelet in the 19th century and is calculated as weight(kg) / height(m)^2. According to the information available at the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), there are four major BMI categories: Underweight when BMI < 18.4; Healthy Weight when 18.5 < BMI < 24.9; Overweight when 25 < BMI < 30; and Obesity when BMI is 30 or greater.

## BMI and chronic disease risk

In recent years, many studies have shown that there are connections between BMI and the risk of chronic conditions like hypertension and diabetes. Our study, using the fitted logistic regression models, included gender, race, and age as control variables, which also found **a significant positive correlation between BMI and the prevalence of diabetes and hypertension.**

Our data comes from the [Hennepin County 2022 SHAPE Survey](https://www.hennepincounty.gov/government/about/research-data), and this project was part of the [UMN Public Health Equity Data Challenge](https://dsai-hub.umn.edu/events/join-umn-public-health-equity-data-challenge-sign-october-14).

## The problem with uniform BMI standards

However, the current BMI does not have race-specific standards and may not accurately reflect health risks for all racial groups. **This brings up an important question: how can we ensure that different racial groups have health screening standards that are fair and relevant to their needs?**

As shown in our analysis, the overall BMI of Asians and Asian Americans is obviously lower than that of other racial groups. Given that we have confirmed a positive correlation between BMI and disease prevalence, this suggests that a lower BMI threshold may be more appropriate for screening Asians for these conditions.

## Tailoring BMI standards by race

At this point, I'm sure many of you are curious about **how we could tailor an equivalent BMI standard that's more suitable for disease screening and prevention for all races.** As we know, developing equivalent BMI standards requires extensive sample data, thorough research, and repeated testing.

In our study, we grouped patients by race and calculated the 15th percentile BMI for diabetes patients within each racial group, representing the equivalent BMI threshold for patients with lower BMIs in that group. The same method was applied for hypertension. This method may offer a useful perspective for establishing a BMI standard that better fits the health needs of each racial group.

Our findings show clear differences in BMI thresholds across racial groups:

- **Asian**: Hypertension 22.0, Diabetes 21.9
- **White**: Hypertension 23.6, Diabetes 24.4
- **Black**: Hypertension 24.0, Diabetes 24.7
- **Hispanic**: Hypertension 25.8, Diabetes 25.7
- **AIAN**: Hypertension 25.1, Diabetes 25.8

## What this means for public health equity

**This tells us that when establishing screening standards like BMI, adjustments should be made to account for the unique characteristics of each racial group, thereby better promoting public health equity.** Without appropriate adjustments, some racial groups may miss out on essential disease screening and prevention due to a lack of alerts triggered by standard thresholds.
