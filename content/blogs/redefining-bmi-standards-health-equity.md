Our team’s question was simple to ask and difficult to answer well: what if a single BMI threshold does not serve every group equally?

During the UMN Public Health Equity Data Challenge, we used R for cleaning, logistic regression, and visualization to explore how disease prevalence thresholds varied across racial groups. The project eventually became both a short report and a blog-style write-up, which forced us to think not only about our analysis, but about how to communicate it responsibly.

## What I learned from leading the team

### Framing the question matters

The quality of a project changes quickly once everyone agrees on the question being answered. Early alignment made the later analysis far more coherent.

### Technical work still needs a narrative

Running a model is not the same as explaining why it matters. We had to move from code to interpretation to implications for screening and equity.

### Public-interest analysis carries an extra responsibility

The point was never to make a flashy claim. It was to show where a standard might deserve more nuance and why evidence should travel carefully.
